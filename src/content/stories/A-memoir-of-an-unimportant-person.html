You may have opened this letter and thought, “What the hell? Why would I write to myself? I’m not worth writing a letter to.” Then, you might have thought, “Wait, this is literally a letter from me. What is happening?” And finally, you’re reading this now, so you thought, “Aw, screw it. I have nothing better to do.”

Treatment is hard. It is hard as hell. You will be told oh-so-many-times that Oh, I get how or why you’re feeling this way, and you’re wrong. You will be told you have to change. You will be told to face your anxiety. And, most scarily, you will be told, “You are not your depression.” And that, that will be the hardest part. Because I—and, honestly, no one else—can say I know you. You are in love with your Depression. You are in love with the meaning you think it gives you. The trifle of a purpose you feel from it. There is an almost monotonous beauty in it. Because every day you wake up and there it is. Depression. At least it will always be there, right?

Well, they’re going to tell you that you’re wrong. And you will be scared. You will be scared to get rid of the only thing that you think makes you special. You will be scared to be willing to change your behavior because then what? You feel better? Man, you've got no identity. Once you lose me, you’re nothing. That’s what Depression is trying to tell you. It’s trying to tell you that you are it and it is you. And that once you both become each other, that then—and only then—can you mean something. Well, it’s a lie. I can tell you. Yes, there is a certain connection you will feel to people here in treatment. You will finally feel connected. Like there is a deep part of you that is the same as them. And, well, that’s your Depression.

I’m telling you to rise above it. Make connections, sure, but don’t fetishize your own sadness. Don’t make it who you are and don’t seek connection because of it. I know you’re lonely, so maybe making the most unhealthy connections seems like the right thing to do. Right? Better than nothing. But let me tell you something: people are here for themselves. People come and go. They’ll say they care about you and, well, maybe they do. But ultimately, they’re there for themselves. You’re here for you. And don’t forget that.

Be willing. You—okay, not you—but this is an expensive program. You get out what you put in. It sounds cliché, but things are cliché for a reason. If you are, for example, told to complete exposures, do them. Face your anxiety. A wise therapist once told me, “If your anxiety is trying to tell you not to do something, you should probably do it.” Excluding the rare dangerous threat, challenge yourself. What can really go wrong? Now, if the catastrophe isn’t as bad as you once thought, just do it. I know you’re scared of social rejection—maybe that’s the best way to put it. And I’m not going to tell you it’s an invalid fear. But I will ask you this. If you feel so lonely, why do you isolate yourself? It doesn’t help, and you know it. Nothing changes unless something changes. And that, obviously, is why you’re going to treatment. Because you know it’s true. They will work on your behaviors, but ultimately nothing will change if you don’t put forth the effort.

So, be willing. Do everything they ask. There is a method to the madness. Good luck!

From,

        Well, You

Life

My life wasn’t hard. If I asked someone to walk a mile in my shoes, they would. Really. Fucking dream. But if I asked someone to walk a mile in my mind, they would lose it.

Everyone left. They were all gone. And I was here, once again in my room, crying. Well, I wasn’t crying. I couldn’t quite cry. I sat in the entombment that was my room and it squeezed me. That stupid blue light. I wish I wasn’t so addicted. But I was because it was there and no one else was. My skin was cold.

You’re the backup.

“Stop,” I wanted to say. But I couldn’t even stand up to myself.

No one loves you. You’re just always there.

I should’ve been studying for…something. It didn’t matter. Who am I doing all this for? Because I sure as hell don’t care anymore. It leads nowhere.

Day in, day out, I’d go to school studying for “Undecided” and, of course, I guess that is who I am. I am a big old question mark, and that’s what people see because that’s what I see, and no one likes an insecure person.

Just an easy footstool. They all left you because you’re not good enough.

The people who were there, my friends, family, every one of them that left. It hurt. But it didn’t hurt me more than I hurt myself. And then I left, too.

A few friends

In this life, everyone only gets a couple of really great friends if they’re lucky. Many many acquaintances may hear you, but few will listen. Even fewer will care, and almost no one will be there.

Listen to those who care and be there for them.

These friends we meet along the journey of life are incredibly valuable and should be treated with the utmost respect, compassion, and dignity. Not everyone gets this subset of a subset of a subset.

Without this subset, nothing in life will be enough. And a roaming mind is a dangerous one, either to itself or to others, so cleanse the mind with good company and give them yours. Identify who is actually there. Not everyone around you actually is, and some are trying to numb themselves with the group.

Alone

The thing about being alone is that it is vitally different from being truly, truly lonely. You could be in a room full of people who don’t care about you or, worse, pretend to care about you. You’re not alone. Except, yes, yes, you are. Which is worse, do you think?

The group of idiots talking about their favorite sports teams and hobbies, and as you look at each one of them, you realize, damn, do I really not have a single ounce of personality, hobby, or relationship to the realm that is humanity? I can look at you and I can’t give a response because I’m just too tired to care and, well, you know they don’t. Or is it the ever-enigmatic mystery that is the rumbling demons when you are truly alone? Secluded. Gone from what people define as normal. Because then I can at least get shit done. I’m not distracted by how normal everyone is and how abnormal I am. Yeah, sure, the voices all tell me I’m worthless and will never amount to anything. Frankly, they’re right.

I never could figure out which was worse.

But loneliness has always sat deep within me. And that shadow haunts me; it scares me. It is me. And, well, I want to do something about it. Really, I do. But I’m frozen and frightened. I am already so miserable and, truthfully, I shouldn’t be dragging anyone down with me.

I could tell myself or even others that there is something wrong with me. I know it traps me. It traps me and well, ironically; it keeps me from changing.

I’m a wallflower. I have always been a wallflower, and that is okay. But, I guess, the bigger issue is that—

It’s more than that,

YOU KNOW?

It’s a bit like I’m here and everyone else is there.

And the experiences, the furnishings, the community, the BEING, the existence, the abode and growth and systematic humanity of all of it is just so odd and strange to me. But everyone else just gets it?

Like, are they not screaming, too? I mean, I can’t hear them; I can only see them. So, I’m asking you. You tell me. Am I missing something?

Is pain the only thing that’s real? It can’t be because I know if I keep going, there will be something else.

Shit, I’m so damn behind in life.

And the thing is I know I’m doing good. Everything is going well. I should be thankful. But I'm not. What. Is. Missing? Like—

Life is Like Sleeping in a Car

Have you ever sat in a parked car and taken a nap? Just to pass the time. It’s odd. Now, this depends on your car, of course, but it is simultaneously the most uncomfortable and the most euphoric, time-stopping experience I have personally ever felt. And it’s just sleep.

Now, the reason I bring this up is because life is a lot like this. Sure, everyone gets dealt a different hand. A different car. A different class, if you will. And we exist in that car so long as we do, sleeping. It’s simultaneously uncomfortable and incredible. Think about that.

Life will throw shit at you that you will never imagine. Technology will exist that I can never surmise. Problems will exist beyond what we bicker about now. But it’s incredible! The fact that we exist at all is a miracle.

So, why does it have to be tough? Nowadays people complain and say, “I never consented to being born!” What are you? God? Do you decide what and who is created? You are, so be.

If you have a bad day, maybe a week, a month, a few years. Well, I’m sorry, not everyone is sleeping in a roomy, nice, cushy fuckin’ car. Some got stuck with a tiny old car with a broken AC. You’ll strain your neck a bit, but at least you get to be part of this incredible journey, right?

The One Others Have

I don’t know how I ended up here. But it was a pretty quick process, my transference to the unfortunate world of The Crazies. Though I guess I couldn’t really argue — I did belong here.

I only remembered the doctor giving me some physical exam — I stripped, the doctor examined my scarred body, and I put my clothes back on, and that was it.

In the facility itself, the eyes were all vacant. Hollow. They were dead and their souls hurt. I don't know what I was looking at or why. I wish someone were next to me to explain the things the man was talking about in plain fucking English and someone would tell me when I could leave.

I hesitated, but I really didn’t want to be here. Disconnected and trapped. Uncomfortable, surrounded yet alone. So, I asked. “It’ll just be over the weekend,” he responded.

I wish I remembered the rules and how things worked and whatnot.

maybe then I'd remember where the bathrooms were.

…but i didn’t give a fuck about showering or changing or washing or hygiene. i wore what i wore and i had no one, and it was only me and these others and the doctors who got paid a grand a day to push Big Pharma’s wishes.

and i never apologized, fuck them. I was just another patient to them. There were no handshakes here. My eyes jagged to the–TICK.

the clock and–TICK.

I twitched. What the fuck? I didn’t remember what the guy next to me had just said, or what slop we were eating, or why I was here.

Some people here had body issues, so we had to drink these disgusting fake chocolate milks that seemed like they were absorbing the construction paper boxes that held them. Why am I being punished for their issues?

But I was also sad. Genuinely sad for everyone who needed to be here. This young and addicts, anorexics, fuck it was depressing. But I didn’t need to be here. Did I?

Does it smell like bleach in this room? Doesn’t matter. We’re all going somewhere else now.

Oh, everyone’s going outside for a smoke. Guess it beats their other addictions.

You know, compared to these people, my life wasn’t that bad. But then why do I want to die? Why is every moment pain? And why can’t I just express it? Why can’t I justify it?

Maybe I need to be here.

No one will listen to me, including myself. Every rumble into the void has to get louder and louder AND LOUDER until the screams annoy them and I’m left alone. I’m happily and depressingly alone. The holes in our hearts cannot be justified, nor can they be mended. Guess they can fucking try, though.

She called my name. “Come join the group.”

I don’t remember a damn thing the fake doctor said in that group because nothing resonated with me because, well, of course, it didn’t. I already know all this garbage. Knowledge isn’t the problem here. I’m the problem. Where’s the magical solution?

Nightfall came, and I must admit, silly as it is, I was more scared now than ever. I wasn’t scared of the dark, but I was scared of the unknown, and I was scared of the loneliness that stared at me in the face, and I was scared that my pain wasn’t real. Because the thing is healing is painful, and escape isn’t.

There was no escape from this fucking place and unfortunately I was in this disorienting room with this Weirdo — he was a Weirdo, I knew this because he was like me and I just wanted this nightmare to be over.

The one nurse came over to take my vitals. I guess they do this daily? A bit egregious, but I didn’t resist. I’m sure some people did. The nurse finished with, “If you need anything at any time tonight or want to talk, just click this button.” She pointed to a button near the tiny nightstand by the terrible bed, and I slept.

There really aren’t many things to do in this damn hole. It was nerve-wracking not knowing how long I’d be here and, honestly, it was boring as fuck. Just hear a lecture about therapy, try to implement it, fail, hate yourself more, and repeat.

I had to admit it, though. My pain had its perks.

I was a creative. My blood ebbed both the best and worst art in the world. My heart pounded with ideas and the blood circulated through me, nervous system activated, hands obedient. I was no longer still, and I was a god, creator of my own universes.

The funny thing about the pain was that it ebbed this creativity. I harvested it. It sanctified it. The pain made the fiction real and the reality null. Have you ever heard of a happy creative? No? That’s because we’re fucking miserable.

I was in this fantastic flow, pencil to paper, casting out the villains that roamed my mind through these drawings, and I guess it made me mad that I could either be totally apathetic and creative or moderately okay and dull as a wet blanket. There was no escaping myself even if I did get better. That is, of course, unless I escape this tragedy of life. Even I knew that wasn’t a real option.

I thought my problems made me special. I mean, it was the medium to my art. It was my core identity. So I held on to them like they were hostages. But, they only made me miserable.

…and FUCK I was still alone, and I could no longer cry. I could no longer express myself outside of these stupid drawings. And no one was there and everyone was there, and just because people don’t see something, it doesn’t mean it’s not there.

I just wanted to be seen.

And the night came. My vitals were taken again, and she was the same nurse. Some time passed. I lied on the miserable excuse for a bed this was, thoughts fluttering like moths that scattered my brain.

I rarely address the shitty liar inside me: I acknowledge him too much, and I let him lie to me. My suffering was my own. My pain was mine to hold and cuddle with in the cold, dark room. The other Crazy lie separately in the other bed across the room. I wondered. How did people sleep, rest, and I mean truly rest in a place like this? It took me a while to get that rest, and maybe I slept. But at some point, I woke up. Or maybe I never slept. Eventually, I fought back against the lie that told me this was all my fault and, with great hesitation, called the nurse.

She came over and I don’t remember what I said. But I know I spilled it all. I might’ve cried. I might’ve cried about the crippling life that I can never justify and the loneliness that was my own fault. And I might’ve cried about the loop I couldn’t escape—not unless someone strapped me to their back and carried me all the way to the finish line. I cried about nothing and everything. I vented because, before now, no one listened. And I vented about the problems that were small but so big to me, and I only wanted a hug because life was a bitch because people can be right by your side, yet it’s so easy to be blind to it. It’s so hard for me to cry, but I might’ve cried that night. And–

Art

It was my creativity that developed, and at some point, it became a tribute to the pain and life circumstances that was this prison. Not that I couldn’t leave. The prison was in my mind. With so much downtime and so much therapy-speak, I became numb to it. It wasn’t helping. And, though there wasn’t much to do, I had paper and pencil. So, I drew

I completed my drawings in some notebook—so real and raw to draw on paper and to feel the absence of the electronic blue light against my face. To feel the pencils’ textures against my hand, pressure against the paper and to exacerbate the lead against it, the colors of the different pencils and the subjective thoughts that can all be interpretations of the real. The sick, which was real, and the scribbles, which were my symbols. The normal, which was now dead. This is why scribbles sell for hundreds or thousands or more in art auctions. Because of the feeling. The feeling that it shouldn’t and the feeling that all those scribbles come from a genius schizoid who can’t—just can’t express themselves in any other way. The mouth that cannot scream. And my colors were interloping, talking for me. The stickmen and the real haunting faces and the abstract, which were real to me—not in form, but in feeling. They sat on paper. They existed. And they were more real than anything.

It certainly was more healing than I knew.

And I wrote of my pain. I wrote too much, and it wasn’t healing. It was self-harm. My journaling was so bad, my therapist told me to stop. She did have a point, but when there were very few people to talk to and—oh yeah, little outside communication—I talked to myself. I wrote to myself. I lied to myself.

Nine Days

Well, I drew, and I wrote and I attended the sessions day in and day out. I identified emotions, found coping mechanisms, and said the “good” words to myself. I did all the things that sort of replicated healing. But, you know, I didn’t heal. I never believed in any of it.

And it was the kings and liars, the money men who had lied to me. I wasn’t here for a weekend.

I was here for over a week. Nine days.

Nine days of simulated comfort, but the worst pain. Nine days of smiling and nodding in frustrating therapy sessions, pretending I was learning regurgitated garbage. Because all this was easier said than done. Especially when motivation leaves, like every hope that never should've been. And the motherfuckers pretended I wasn’t doing anything. Like I wasn’t trying. You try being pushed on every imaginable anti-whatever medication until you can’t remember a damn thing.

The nine days didn’t fly by, but the thought of being stuck there with mushy food under the watchful eye, being told what to do and when to do it with the only joy of smoking—I don’t even smoke—was a confusion and malice that wasn’t helpful. It was like prison. Except I didn’t stab someone to deserve this!

“You’re not getting better.”

No shit. I thought. “But, doctor, I believe I am getting better.” I couldn’t even fake the lie through my teeth, and my void smile twitched, numb. I couldn't even look at him.

“In fact, you’re getting worse.”

You put me here. You put me somewhere with a bunch of suicidal people and drug addicts. Of course I’m not getting better. An alcoholic doesn't go to NA. The system is broken, and all you do is shove everyone into it, regardless of what they need. You suck them dry and leave them medicated, leave them to chance, and keep yourselves rich. What do you want from me?

I turned my head to look at the scary doctor. He looked down at my weak eyes as if I were some wounded little animal he wanted to put out of its misery. He should have. It was better than his dreadful stare.

I can go from system to system and all that really mattered was that they could squeeze me until I finally croaked.

“You can go home; you’re here voluntarily.”

Yeah, I’ll do that.

The idiot continued. “But we don’t want something bad to end up happening to you. I’m recommending this.”

He handed me a brochure and I couldn’t even make out the words because I was tired and, damn, were these doctors insistent!

The idiot kept talking about what I assumed the brochure went over. I didn’t bother reading it because I couldn’t and, frankly, even though it was my decision, it also wasn’t. The words that flowed from the doctor's lips were irrelevant. I knew what would happen.

The Other Place

I still hadn’t showered and, true to form, felt dirty. But not in smell or grease or BO or the endless shitty smoke accumulated on this same shirt I’ve been wearing for the past nine days. I felt dirty because, genuinely; I felt bad for all the Weirdos. I hadn’t really known any of them in any capacity, but some stories they would tell me and some things I’d see made my problems feel so small. Some people really are victims of circumstance. Who am I to complain? Who am I to leave them behind?

Well, unfortunately, I had to. I left a lot of things behind under the guise of working on myself. Hopefully, in one breath and at one moment it would all come together from simple knowledge and understanding to a tale of victory. But for now, here I was. In a new prison. This one was certainly homier. In fact, after finishing whatever process it was that I had gone through to get here, I established that, yes, I was here until they decided I didn’t have to be here and, paradoxically, I was here “voluntarily.” I observed the inside of my new fake home. Its organs. The nurses. The therapists. The others like me.

They sat specifically in an open-style loft area. Vague carpeting and therapists monitored to make sure no one would up and leave or do something stupid. 24/7.

Before I could awkwardly join them and acclimate, the damn king came knocking at my door.

Connection

People look down too much. Either mentally or physically. We’re programmed to look down on ourselves, at others, and at the devices and artificial building blocks that control our lives. We’re programmed to care about work. Our work, others’ work. Every stupid little detail. Once we let go of the silly things, we can cut deep. We can look inwards. Inwards to ourselves and, with kind permission, inwards to others and to the realities of the world surrounding us.

The truth is that the current world is built on an artificial stampede of attention-seeking zeroes and ones, none of which matter. What matters is in front of you. It’s the fleeting and the current and the staying and the real and the promise of feeling. The promise that you’ll always be you and that the only reality and promise in life isn’t taxes and death. It is change. Change for the better or for the worse, that’s on you. Just look at the world. Not its appearance.

“Sometimes we get attached to these things. We get attached to these emotions and to these feelings. But you are not your feelings. Your feelings are valid, and I understand the pain you’re feeling. And I’m sorry. I’m with you.

“But don’t let them control you. Feel them; experience them. But let them go. You get to control the dog. It’ll always be there, but you get to control it. You get to love yourself. And hurting doesn’t make you special. You’re special because of the talents you have and who you are and the light you can bring to the world. The laughter you bring everyone. The care you give. And I know you care because you cared enough to come here. But, remember, being hurt doesn’t make you special. It just hurts you.”

The therapist finished, and before I could even think or respond or tell her she was wrong — she was not — she asked, “What do you do to help yourself feel better when you’re anxious or depressed?”

I paused. I looked around the small, frankly crowded office, scattered with stacks of redundant paperwork. I sat in the wooden chair that was not conducive to relaxation or feeling at home.

This wasn’t home, and for a while I forgot that. This place, these people, these words, these routines. They were a band-aid. I had to do something. I wasn’t doing anything. I was this passive blob that let anything take over. I performed the activities ritualistically, but not with my heart. I’d let any idea, any thought, any evil self-contempt, any judgement of the self or otherwise take over.

“I…I don’t know,” I hesitated, the words barely leaving my breath.

Anxiety

The worst thing about this place wasn’t the fact that I was here. It was the fact that I was anxious to be here. And I was stuck. And the people were unfamiliar. I always choked on words and everything was just–they’re judging you.

It was hard to talk, and hard to ask for things, and hard to even pretend I was a person and not a dissociated soul floating in space until the reaper took me. And that perceptual cycle only grew my anxiety and my almost-phobia of people. Or at least approaching people. I mean, seriously. How am I supposed to talk to people? What do people talk about, and what do people say, and how do people give a fuck about themselves or others, and how do people assimilate in a broken world?

There was this other therapist who judged me for being terrible at this specific aspect of life—right, like I needed people. But I did. That’s why I was here.

“Sometimes we have thoughts that don’t reflect reality,” this therapist said. “Sometimes our minds like to catastrophize and tell us the worst-case scenario.” He continued.

“I–”

“This adaptation exists so that we can survive. Or that was its original intention. But bears aren’t out and about hunting us anymore.

“If you talk to someone, chances are they’ll talk back and things won’t be so bad. When do you feel your anxiety coming up?”

I wanted to smash his face in for interrupting me. Smug little bastard. You’ve been rejected and hurt. You can’t do anything about it. It’s better to be alone.

“Do you find the anxiety coming before an interaction, during the interaction or afterwards?” He finished.

“Um…” I hesitated. It’s a trap. “I think it’s more before. Sometimes during.”

“Well, I want you to try approaching people and see what happens.” He said.

Exposed

To most people, even here, approaching people and talking came naturally. I…I just couldn't. Well, it was not so much the talking as it was…

the agitation

Of asking. Imposing. Being. I couldn't even ask for a fork without sweating uncontrollably.

I remember standing around this group of people. The idea of intervening and saying anything…

your ideas are stupid. Keep your fucking mouth shut.

The golf ball was always in my mouth and I wanted to vomit it out. I wanted to just be, but this simple task and the simple words were impossible. I was itchy.

“Can I. Have, um…” I drifted. I don't remember what I even asked for. Permission to speak?

You idiot.

Meditations

I always thought meditation and mindfulness were such weird things. We’d do them once, maybe twice a day. To me, it was a cult obsession. Like, oh, let’s hypnotize ourselves and pretend we’re okay, and we’ll suddenly be okay. I had no idea what everyone else thought about it, but to me it was only a temporary lie.

“Today you’re feeling a little stressed.” It was a British voice on YouTube over some blue sky and calming music. I looked at the television that rolled into the loft as if it was now my master.

We sat in a circle, the voice filling the room.

It just made me sleepy.

And I guess that was magical in itself. My problems were gone when I was asleep. They lulled away. And I was here. Not stressing about the future or eternally bleeding about the past. I was now. Now and without thought.

And it wasn't like anything changed. I was in the community and we were all self-hypnotizing the thoughts and trauma away. Not that they were gone, but they were dormant. The sounds and the nature and the moment were here, and it was now. It was us.

One Thing At One Time And One Place And Never Anywhere Else

I always thought this would be it. I couldn’t help myself. And, though they gave me everything, every tool and every possible medication and every word over and over and over again, I was stubborn. I tried to fight myself to win. But, you can’t fight yourself. I sat and checked the boxes. But we're not grocery shopping. I went through the medications. Drugs couldn’t control my thoughts. I did everything, but nothing stuck. I did everything. But—you’re gonna fail.

I never believed I’d succeed. This was it. I could never be real. Even when I left, I’d no longer make it in the world. I’d never graduate. I’d never make friends. I’d never make connections. This structured 12 to 12 where I was babied–this was all I had and all that made sense.

“Things can change so quickly.” It wasn’t a therapist who said that. It was the community of people who understood and possibly even cared for my small problems. They said those words with very little elaboration, but I saw their souls, and it was real. And with so much truth and care, it stuck with me. More than anything any therapist had ever said. More than anything anyone had ever said. That with work and with help, there was always hope.

Fin

I remember some of these people in great detail and some with no detail at all. This place was a labyrinth, and we were stuck here together. We were healing together, and that’s all that mattered.

What’s always confused me about the world we deemed society was all the pity jealousies and attachments people had about such irrelevant topics. Irrelevant events. And, yeah, ‘irrelevant’ is subjective. Yeah, I’ve become detached. I’ve become cut off. But not before The Normal snipped the string.

Here there was none of that. Just the real and raw. Even if we didn’t know everything about each other or know each other for very long, we knew what mattered. We felt comfort in knowing we weren’t alone. That there were others like us.

We didn’t just hear each other; we listened. And we healed. We healed because it wasn’t just talk and words. They were the real words and real ears of people who understood.

I wondered about all the people in here and, I guess, to a degree I got to know them a lot more than some of my best friends. Especially the friends who weren’t there for me. Because it wasn’t just emotional bonding. To me, it was growth through struggle with strangers. It was cutting past the mundane and getting to what's real. I was less of an outsider–

but you are an outsider

I was amid this group that I got to know and before anyone could ask me what my favorite whatever-who-cares is or what I’m going to school for, or what I enjoy or what my hobbies were or—I frankly never cared. And, luckily, neither did they. Because before they could ask me or I could ask them, I could see the hurt and feel the hurt and even though it was all different for all of us, here, we were all equal.

you’re not equal, you’re less than

The whole time I just wanted someone to say, “I don't understand, but I do understand.” To have someone say with true, real perception and acuity that it's going to be okay. That, despite it all, I am here. That everything was real, and I wasn't broken. That everything would be okay.

And, at some point, I’d become part of the group that was there when I wasn't. I’d share openly, and they’d share openly, and we were all real. And though we were different, we were the same.

It wasn’t a group to sit in and hide from yourself, to be another drop of water in the sea and blend in. It was a bloody sea, and we suffered. We cried, we laughed, and, most importantly, we celebrated our successes. We celebrated equally. It was the first and last group I was part of. And though I suffered, and the distress was real, so was the rebirth.
